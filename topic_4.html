What is Infection prevention and control?

According to the World Health Organization (WHO), infection prevention and control (IPC) is a scientific approach and effective solution aimed to stop infections-related harm to patients and medical staff. Although it is a subset of epidemiology, but also crucial to the research of infectious diseases, the social sciences, and global health in general.


The major issue of public health for patient safety and the growth of the healthcare system is effective IPC . Effective IPC measures are the basis for the prevention of healthcare-associated infections (HAI), epidemics (such as the Ebola virus disease outbreak of 2013–2016), and pandemics of global concern (e.g. as the coronavirus disease of 2019 and the flu pandemic of 2009). "Access to health care services designed and managed to minimise the risks of avoidable HAI for patients and health care workers is a basic human right," is the guiding principle on WHO's Core Component of IPC.


Strengthening global IPC is essential to combat HAI, antimicrobial resistance, and to respond to disease outbreaks . According to a 2021 study that examined the WHO's Core Components of the IPC, most participating nations have IPC rules and activities in place; however, few have set aside the necessary funds to implement the programmes. Transitioning from the planning phase to the implementation and monitoring phases is crucial, especially in low-income nations.


The Spread of Infectious Disease

An infection is defined as the successful transmission of pathogenic microorganisms , such as bacteria, viruses, parasites or fungi that are spread:

1. Directly:
- Direct exposure to infectious agent in environment
- Through body fluids
- During childbirth from mother to foetus (transplacental / perinatal)
- From person to person
- Through respiratory droplets (for example, coughing or sneezing)
2. Indirectly:
- Airborne
Example: Tuberculosis
- Biological: Vector or Intermediate host
Example: Zika Virus
- Mechanical: Vector or Vehicle
Example: Plague - transmission of Yersinia Pestis by fleas

Know the Signs and Symptoms of Infection

During your chemotherapy treatment, your body may not be able to fight off infections like it used to. If you have low white blood cell counts, you need to be concerned about infection symptoms. Hospitalisation or even death may result from an infection during treatment.

Call your doctor right away if you notice any of the following signs and symptoms of an infection:

- Fever
- Sweating
- Fatigue
- Weakness
- Localized pain
- Swelling
- Redness
- Warmth in affected area
- Pus or discharge
- Persistent cough
- Sore throat
- Nasal congestion
- Runny nose
- Nausea
- Vomiting
- Diarrhea
- Abdominal pain
- Rash

Controlling Infectious Diseases Within Communities

Infection control and prevention is a global concern. There are many protocols and suggestions that can be followed to reduce the spread of infection between individuals, within a group, and worldwide. The identification of vulnerable populations, including children, the elderly, and people with long-term medical concerns, may additionally provide pertinent policies for their protection.

The first step when looking at infection control can start at the community level by changing behaviour , including:

1. Maintain Good Hand Hygiene

Frequent Handwashing: Wash your hands regularly with soap and water for at least 20 seconds before eating.

Hand Sanitizer: Carry and use alcohol-based hand sanitizer (with at least 60% alcohol) when soap and water are not available.

2. Maintain Physical Distance

Social Distancing: Keep a safe distance (at least 2 meter) from people who are not from your household, especially in crowded places.

Avoid Close Contact: Refrain from close physical contact like handshakes, hugs, and kisses.

3. Wear Masks Appropriately

Mask Usage: Wear a mask in crowded places, on public transport, and in situations where maintaining physical distance is challenging.

Proper Mask Handling: Ensure your mask covers both your nose and mouth and avoid touching the mask while wearing it.

4. Clean and Disinfect

Surface Cleaning: Regularly clean and disinfect frequently touched surfaces, such as mobile phones, keys, and bags.

Sanitize Personal Spaces: If using public facilities like gym equipment or picnic tables, sanitize them before use.

Other steps that can be taken to control the spread within communities include environmental measures such as:

1. Improve Water and Sanitation

Access to Clean Water: Ensure that all community members have access to safe and clean drinking water.

Sanitation Facilities: Build and maintain proper sanitation facilities, including toilets and waste disposal systems, to prevent contamination and the spread of waterborne diseases.

2. Vaccination Programs

Routine Immunization: Maintain high vaccination coverage through regular immunization programs for preventable diseases.

Outbreak Response: Implement targeted vaccination campaigns during outbreaks to quickly control the spread of the disease.

3. Improve Air Quality

Reduce Indoor Air Pollution: Promote the use of cleaner fuels and efficient stoves for cooking and heating to reduce indoor air pollution.

Ventilation: Ensure proper ventilation in homes, schools, and workplaces to reduce the transmission of airborne diseases.

4. Control Vectors and Pests

Insect Control Programs: Conduct regular insecticide spraying and eliminate standing water to reduce mosquito populations and prevent diseases like malaria and dengue.

Rodent Control: Implement measures to control rodent populations, such as proper waste management and use of traps, to prevent diseases like leptospirosis and plague.

For more information, you can visit to National Cancer Institute's Infection and Neutropenia During Cancer Treatment and the BMC Infectious Diseases to get further information.
